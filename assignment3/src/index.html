Do Androids dream of electric sheep?

About

"Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?" is a science fiction novel by Philip K.Dick, first published in 1968.
Set in a post-apocalyptic future, the story revolves around Rick Deckard, a bounty hunter tasked with "retiring" rogue androids that have escaped to Earth from distant off-world colonies. The novel explores themes of identity, empathy, and the nature of humanity in a world where technology blurs the line between human and artificial intelligence. In this future society, owning a living animal is considered a status symbol, as most real animals have become extinct due to radiation from World War Terminus. To compensate, people keep mechanical animals, known as "electric sheep". As Deckard hunts down the androids, he questions what it means to be human and the morality of his actions. The story delves into the philosophical implications of artificial intelligence, asking whether empathy and emotions are exclusive to humans or if they can be programmed into machines.



A Literary Analysis

Acceptance vs. Selectivity
Dick explores hypocrisy through Mercerism, a belief system promoting empathy. Yet, its followers are exclusive, rejecting marginalized individuals like chickenheads. Despite their empathy, chickenheads, victims of World War Terminus radiation, are barred from migrating to Mars or integrating into society. Ironically, John Isidore, a chickenhead, exhibits profound empathy. When he presents a living spider, prized in a world of extinct animals, to androids, they mutilate it. Isidore, despite his cognitive limitations, euthanizes the spider to end its suffering, prioritizing compassion over profit. His action highlights the irony of Mercerism's teachings. Despite being excluded from society, Isidore embodies true empathy, challenging societal norms. Through his character, Dick critiques the hypocrisy inherent in Mercerism's selective empathy and societal exclusivity.

Truth vs. Fiction
Dick also delves into the deception surrounding Mercerism, a religion aiming to unify humanity through empathy and mood organs, connecting individuals to a collective consciousness. The mood organs underscore the divide between reality and illusion. Users enter a virtual reality via the empathy box, experiencing events with real-world consequences. However, revelations expose Mercer as a fraud, his image fabricated by Hollywood. Despite this, Mercerism persists, offering solace in an increasingly artificial world. Mercer, though exposed, remains a messianic figure, uniting humanity under a spiritual guise. Rick Deckard, grappling with empathy towards androids, confronts Mercer, who acknowledges the hypocrisy but urges him to continue hunting. This underscores the existential struggle of being human in a digital age, where authenticity is scarce and empathy is a rare commodity.

Reality vs. Artificiality
Dick also examines the blurred distinction between living and nonliving entities. William Mercer propagates the belief that empathy distinguishes humans from androids, attributing value to life. However, androids challenge this notion by experiencing empathetic emotions themselves. For instance, during Rick Deckard's hunt for androids, he encounters Roy Baty, who exhibits anguish when Deckard shoots Irmgard Baty. Deckard observes Roy's love for her, indicating a form of empathy. Additionally, while androids prioritize self-preservation, they also show concern for each other's well-being. In contrast, humans are conditioned to lack empathy for androids, leading to a decline in their own emotional capacity. Phil Resch exemplifies this by killing androids without remorse, deriving pleasure from it. Similarly, Deckard's pursuit of androids stems from self-interest rather than empathy. He seeks bounties to afford real animals, enhancing his social status, devoid of genuine concern for others.

Conclusion
Ultimately, the novel delves into humanity's quest for significance in a decaying world, where empathy emerges as the primary unifying force. Dick explores various secondary themes, such as the dichotomy between reality and artificiality, individual versus collective desires, and the struggle between truth and fiction. These themes underscore the hypocrisy underlying people's efforts to preserve unity. He suggests that a shared belief system is essential for humanity's survival, regardless of its validity. Characters often act in ways contrary to the tenets of their religion, yet find solace in it amid bleak circumstances. Despite knowing its falsehoods, they cling to hope offered by these principles, which have become their reality.

Symbolism of the protagonist Rick Deckard

Paradoxical Humanity
As a Blade Runner tasked with "retiring" androids, Rick Deckard symbolizes existential questions about human identity. He grapples with the task of judging and eliminating non-human beings by human standards, leading to doubts about his own humanity.

Ethical Reflection
Deckard's story provides an exploration of ethical dilemmas and profound moral choices. By eliminating androids, Deckard confronts the contradictory nature of his role and his ethical responsibilities.

Blurry Boundaries between Reality and Dream
Deckard's narrative unfolds in a world where the boundaries between reality and dream are blurred. This challenges readers t question their own perceptions and understanding of reality.

Meaning of Humanity
Deckard raises questions about whether androids can experience emotions. His journey suggests the possibility that androids may possess emotions and consciousness comparable to humans.

Backstory

The backstory of "Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?" originates from Philip K. Dick's research for his novel "The Man in the High Castle". While delving into diaries seized after World War II, Dick encountered accounts of unimaginable atrocities committed by individuals devoid of empathy. These experiences led him to conceptualize beings lacking empathy as "androids," setting the stage for his exploration of empathy as a central theme in his novel. In these diaries, Dick came across a chilling entry where a Nazi officer complained about being kept awake by the cries of starving children, viewing their suffering as a mere inconvenience. This lack of empathy struck Dick profoundly, prompting him to contemplate the essence of humanity and empathy. This exploration of empathy forms the crux of "Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?" as Dick delves into the nature of empathy and its significance in defining humanity. The novel revolves around the blurred line between humans and androids, questioning what it truly means to be empathetic and whether empathy is essential for being considered a living being. The protagonist, Rick Deckard, embarks on a journey that challenges his understanding of empathy and his own humanity as he encounters androids who may possess empathetic capabilities. Through Deckard's experiences, Dick prompts readers to reflect on the complexities of empathy and its role in shaping our perceptions of ourselves and others.
